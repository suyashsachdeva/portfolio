Why a Chaotic Loss Landscape Still Works

Training a neural network means walking downhill on a surface that turns out to be jagged at every scale — a fractal. Here is what that geometry is, and why optimisation succeeds anyway.

By Suyash Sachdeva / Deep Learning · Optimisation / 14 min read / June 2026
In one paragraph

A neural network is trained by minimising a loss function — in principle, just rolling downhill until the error is small. But the surface we roll on is not a smooth bowl. It is a high-dimensional landscape of valleys, ridges, plateaus and sharp pits, and recent work argues that it is fractal: zoom into any region and similar complexity reappears at finer and finer scales. This note explains that idea in plain language and shows why it helps make sense of unstable training, flat minima, and the strange reliability of stochastic gradient descent.

Adapted from “Fractal Geometry in Neural Network Loss Landscapes: Mathematical Foundations and Optimization Implications.”1
01 — The surface we descend

What a loss landscape actually is

A modern neural network has millions — sometimes billions — of parameters. Every possible setting of those parameters is a single point in an enormous space, and at each point the loss function reports one number: how badly the network is doing.

Collect all of those points together with their loss values and you get the loss landscape — the shape formed by every parameter setting and its corresponding error. Training is the act of moving across this shape, trying to reach a low point.

For a tiny model the landscape can be a clean convex bowl with one obvious bottom. Deep networks are nothing like that. Their landscapes are riddled with sharp minima, flat minima, saddle points and unstable boundaries, and these features connect to one another in complicated ways (Figure 1). Instead of one tidy minimum, there are many — some reachable, some not.

02 — Complexity that never goes away

Why fractals enter the picture

A fractal is a structure that stays complex no matter how far you zoom in. A coastline is the classic example: from orbit and from a few metres away it looks equally crinkled. The claim explored in the source paper is that neural-network loss landscapes behave similarly — zooming into a small region does not make it simple.1

More precisely, these landscapes are described as multifractal: different parts have different degrees of roughness.1 A rough region is crowded with sharp local minima and large fluctuations; a smoother region holds broad, gentle basins (Figure 2). The surface is not uniformly bumpy — its bumpiness itself changes from place to place.

This single observation explains one of deep learning's most reliable empirical findings: models that finish training in flatter regions tend to perform better on data they have never seen. The geometry, not luck, is doing the work.

03 — Measuring roughness

The Hölder exponent, in plain terms

To talk about roughness precisely we need a number for it. That number is the Hölder exponent, written H. Intuitively it answers a simple question: as you take a tiny step, how violently can the loss change?

  • Small H → a rough region. Tiny steps can cause large jumps in loss; the surface is jagged, full of sharp minima.
  • Large H → a smooth region. The loss changes gently; minima are broad and stable.

In a multifractal landscape this exponent is not fixed — it varies from one location to the next (Figure 3). So an optimiser does not travel across a surface of uniform texture. It moves through a space where the very roughness underfoot keeps changing, which is part of why training can feel calm in one phase and turbulent in another.

04 — The same shape at every scale

Self-similarity, and why it matters

The defining fractal property is self-similarity: small regions resemble larger ones in structure.1 For a loss landscape this has a practical sting. When training appears to settle into a small basin, that basin may itself contain smaller basins, ridges and unstable paths — and those, in turn, contain still finer structure (Figure 4).

Optimisation is not just finding a minimum. It is navigating a multi-scale geometric object whose detail never runs out.

05 — Where the geometry comes from

How architecture shapes the landscape

The shape of the loss surface is not arbitrary — it is sculpted by how the network is built. Depth, activation functions, skip connections, width and normalisation all leave their fingerprints on the geometry.1

Depth

Deeper networks generally have more complex landscapes. Very deep models without shortcuts can become extremely non-convex and unstable to train.

Skip connections

Residual shortcuts (as in ResNets) make the surface far more navigable, reducing the chance of getting trapped in chaotic regions.2

Activation function

ReLU is not smooth at zero, so it stamps sharp, piecewise kinks into the surface. Smoother activations yield different basin structure.

Width & normalisation

Wider, over-parameterised networks tend to smooth the landscape; batch normalisation can stabilise the descent through it.

The same loss profile, then, can be made friendlier or more hostile by design choices alone (Figure 5). A skip connection is not just an engineering trick — it is a way of reshaping the geometry into something an optimiser can actually cross.2

06 — Moving through the mess

Optimisation as a trajectory

Training is a path through this landscape. Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) does not glide smoothly downhill in a straight line — it interacts with roughness, curvature and noise. In jagged regions it explores aggressively, bouncing around; in smoother regions it slows and settles into a wide basin.

The noise that comes from using small random batches is not merely a nuisance. It can help the optimiser jump out of narrow, sharp pits and find broader basins instead — one reason SGD generalises better than its smoother, full-batch cousin. The geometry rewards an explorer that knows when to settle.

07 — Fractals beyond the weights

Ragged boundaries in hyperparameter space

The fractal view is not confined to the model's parameters. It can also appear in the space of hyperparameters — especially the learning rate. Small changes to the learning rate, batch size or initialisation can flip training from stable convergence to outright divergence.6

This is why hyperparameter tuning so often feels unpredictable. The frontier between "this configuration trains" and "this one blows up" need not be a smooth line — it can be irregular and scale-dependent, a fractal boundary in its own right (Figure 6).6

08 — Flat versus sharp

Why flat minima generalise

Perhaps the most useful practical consequence is the link between flat minima and generalisation. A sharp minimum can fit the training data perfectly yet sit on a knife-edge: nudge the parameters slightly and the loss leaps. A flat minimum is forgiving — small perturbations barely change the loss, so the model is more robust to the inevitable mismatch between training and test data (Figure 7).34

The fractal framework gives this a geometric reading: flat minima live in the smoother, large-H regions of the landscape, while sharp minima cluster in the rough ones. Seeking flatness is, in effect, seeking the gentle parts of a fractal surface.

09 — One geometry, many phenomena

What this perspective unifies

The pleasing thing about the fractal picture is how many separate observations it ties together. Edge-of-stability training,5 mode connectivity between solutions,7 the success of flat minima,3 anomalous diffusion of SGD, implicit regularisation, and the maddening sensitivity to hyperparameters6 all become facets of the same underlying object (Figure 8).

10 — Takeaway

Complexity is the feature, not the bug

The loss landscape of a neural network is not merely complicated; it may be a genuinely multifractal object whose roughness, curvature and dynamics vary across scales. Read that way, complexity stops looking like an obstacle and starts looking like part of the explanation.

Neural networks do not succeed because their landscapes are simple. They succeed because optimisers — noisy, stubborn, and surprisingly well-suited to the terrain — are able to navigate landscapes that have no business being navigable.

Practical lessons
  1. Learning rate is geometry-dependent. The same rate behaves differently in rough versus smooth regions — expect to retune as training moves.
  2. Skip connections reshape the surface. They are a tool for making the landscape trainable, not just a depth hack.
  3. Flat minima are meaningful, not lucky. They correspond to smoother fractal regions and tend to generalise.
  4. Instability is expected. Networks often train near the edge of stability, where small changes have outsized effects.
  5. SGD works by exploring then settling. Its noise helps escape sharp pits and find wide basins.
